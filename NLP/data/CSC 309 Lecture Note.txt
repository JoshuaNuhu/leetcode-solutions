AN OVERVIEW OF ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE
What is Artificial Intelligence?
It is a branch of Computer Science that pursues creating the computers or machines as intelligent as human beings. It is the science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent computer programs. It is related to the similar task of using computers to understand human intelligence, but AI does not have to confine itself to methods that are biologically observable.
Definition: Artificial Intelligence is the study of how to make computers do things, which, at the moment, people do better. According to the father of Artificial Intelligence, John McCarthy, it is "The science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent computer programs". Artificial Intelligence is a way of making a computer, a computer-controlled robot, or a software think intelligently, in the similar manner the intelligent humans think. AI is accomplished by studying how human brain thinks and how humans learn, decide, and work while trying to solve a problem, and then using the outcomes of this study as a basis of developing intelligent software and systems. It has gained prominence recently due to big data, or the increase in speed, size and variety of data businesses are now collecting. AI can perform tasks such as identifying patterns in the data more efficiently than humans, enabling businesses to gain more insight out of their data. From a business perspective AI is a set of very powerful tools, and methodologies for using those tools to solve business problems. From a programming perspective, AI includes the study of symbolic programming, problem solving, and search.

The Birth of AI: Tracing the Origins
The journey of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a fascinating one, tracing its roots back to the mid-20th century. To truly appreciate the complexity and potential of AI as we know it today, it is essential to understand its origins and the key milestones that shaped its development.
The Dawn of AI: The Dartmouth Conference
In 1956, a group of pioneering scientists gathered at Dartmouth College for what would become known as the Dartmouth Conference. This event is often hailed as the birthplace of AI. The conference brought together researchers from various fields, including mathematics, computer science, and psychology, to discuss the possibility of creating machines that could simulate human intelligence.
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon were among the key participants. They proposed the idea of a machine that could learn, reason, and solve problems, much like a human. This conference laid the groundwork for what would become a rapidly evolving field. Early AI Programs: Logic Theorist and General Problem Solver
Following the Dartmouth Conference, the first AI programs began to emerge. One of the earliest and most notable was the Logic Theorist, developed by Allen Newell and Herbert A. Simon in 1956. This program was designed to prove mathematical theorems using logical reasoning. It demonstrated that machines could perform tasks that required human-like thinking.
Another significant development was the General Problem Solver (GPS), also created by Newell and Simon. GPS was an attempt to create a program that could solve a wide range of problems using heuristics, or rules of thumb. Although it had limitations, GPS was a crucial step towards developing more versatile AI systems.
The Rise of Expert Systems
In the 1970s and 1980s, AI research shifted towards developing expert systems. These systems were designed to replicate the decision-making ability of a human expert in a specific domain. One of the most famous expert systems was MYCIN, developed by Edward Shortliffe at Stanford University. MYCIN was used to diagnose blood infections and suggest appropriate antibiotics.

Expert systems like MYCIN demonstrated the potential of AI to assist in complex decision-making processes. However, they also highlighted the limitations of rule-based systems, as they struggled to handle uncertainties and ambiguities.
The AI Winter and the Rebirth of Neural Networks
The late 1980s and early 1990s saw a period known as the "AI Winter," characterized by a significant reduction in funding and interest in AI research. Many expert systems failed to live up to their promises, leading to disillusionment among investors and researchers.
However, during this period, a critical development was taking place: the revival of neural networks. Inspired by the structure and function of the human brain, neural networks consist of layers of interconnected nodes that can learn from data. The introduction of backpropagation, a method for training neural networks, revolutionized the field and laid the foundation for the modern era of AI. The Modern Era of AI: Deep Learning and Big Data
The 21st century has witnessed an explosion of AI research and applications, driven by advancements in computing power, the availability of big data, and the development of deep learning techniques. Deep learning, a subset of machine learning, involves training artificial neural networks with multiple layers to recognize patterns and make decisions.
Applications of AI have become ubiquitous, from virtual assistants like Siri and Alexa to self-driving cars and advanced medical diagnostics. The ability of AI to process and analyze vast amounts of data has transformed industries and opened up new possibilities for innovation.
Key Figures and Contributions
Throughout the history of AI, several key figures have made significant contributions to the field. Alan Turing, often considered the father of computer science, laid the theoretical groundwork for AI with his concept of the Turing machine and the Turing test. Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio are modern pioneers who have made groundbreaking advancements in deep learning. Ethical Considerations and the Future of AI
As AI continues to evolve, it is crucial to consider the ethical implications of its development and deployment. Issues such as bias in algorithms, privacy concerns, and the potential impact on employment must be addressed to ensure that AI benefits society as a whole.
The future of AI holds immense potential, with ongoing research in areas such as explainable AI, quantum computing, and human-AI collaboration. By understanding the origins and evolution of AI, we can better appreciate the challenges and opportunities that lie ahead.
In conclusion, the birth of AI is a story of innovation, collaboration, and perseverance. From the Dartmouth Conference to the modern era of deep learning, the field has come a long way. As we continue to explore the possibilities of AI, it is essential to build upon the foundations laid by the pioneers who dared to imagine a world where machines could think and learn like humans.

The introduction of machine learning in the 1980s marked another major milestone. The 1990s and 2000s witnessed exponential growth in computing power and data availability, fueling rapid advancements in AI. During this period, we saw the rise of neural networks and deep learning, techniques that mimic the structure and function of the human brain. These technologies enabled AI systems to tackle complex tasks with unprecedented accuracy.
In recent years, AI has made remarkable strides in areas like autonomous vehicles, healthcare diagnostics, and natural language understanding. These achievements would not have been possible without the foundational work and milestones we've discussed. As we continue to build upon this legacy, the future of AI holds even greater promise.

AI TECHNIQUES
AI techniques are the methods, strategies, and tools used to build intelligent systems.
a. Search Techniques
Search is fundamental to AI problem-solving.
i. Uninformed (Blind) Search: Breadth-First Search (BFS), Depth-First Search (DFS)
ii. Informed (Heuristic) Search: A*, Greedy Best-First Search These techniques explore possible states to reach a goal.
b. Knowledge Representation

Knowledge representation deals with how information is stored and used. Common methods include:
i. Logical representations (Propositional and Predicate Logic)
ii. Semantic Networks
iii. Frames
iv. Ontologies
c. Reasoning and Inference
Reasoning allows AI systems to:
i. Draw conclusions from known facts
ii. Make decisions under uncertainty Types include:
* Deductive reasoning
* Inductive reasoning
* Probabilistic reasoning (e.g., Bayesian inference)
d. Machine Learning Techniques
Machine learning enables systems to learn from data.
i. Supervised Learning: classification and regression
ii. Unsupervised Learning: clustering and association
iii. Reinforcement Learning: learning through rewards and penalties
e. Heuristic Techniques
Heuristics are experience-based rules that:
i. Reduce search space
ii. Improve efficiency
iii. Provide approximate solutions when exact solutions are impractical

INTRODUCTION TO INTELLIGENT AGENTS
Intelligent agents are entities that use sensors to perceive the environment, make a decision, and act upon that information using actuators. An intelligent agent could be a robot, machine, or even a human or an animal. The sensor that takes in the environment could be a camera, rain sensor, or nose. The intelligent agent processes this information and decides to act upon it. The action could be saving a video, turning off a sprinkler system, or looking for the nearest pizza shop. The actuator is the part that takes action. Intelligent agents make decisions in real time, and the rate of success versus error varies. The purpose of an intelligent agent is to respond to the environment around it.
Intelligent agents are found throughout many aspects of life. Refrigerators sense temperature in order to determine when additional cooling is needed. Smoke detectors sense the presence of smoke before sounding an alarm. A dog that barks as a stranger approach the door also counts as an intelligent agent. Complex intelligent agents include the SpaceX Dragon spacecraft and self-driving cars. These intelligent agents take information from many different sensors, make real-time decisions, and adjust actions based on the information.



Key features of an AI agent
As explained above, while the key features of an AI agent are reasoning and acting, more features have evolved over time.
i. Reasoning: This core cognitive process involves using logic and available information to draw conclusions, make inferences, and solve problems. AI agents with strong reasoning capabilities can analyze data, identify patterns, and make informed decisions based on evidence and context.
ii. Acting: The ability to take action or perform tasks based on decisions, plans, or external input is crucial for AI agents to interact with their environment and achieve goals. This can include physical actions in the case of embodied AI, or digital actions like sending messages, updating data, or triggering other processes.
iii. Observing: Gathering information about the environment or situation through perception or sensing is essential for AI agents to understand their context and make informed decisions. This can involve various forms of perception, such as computer vision, natural language processing, or sensor data analysis.
iv. Planning: Developing a strategic plan to achieve goals is a key aspect of intelligent behavior. AI agents with planning capabilities can identify the necessary steps, evaluate potential actions, and choose the best course of action based on available information and desired outcomes. This often involves anticipating future states and considering potential obstacles.
v. Collaborating: Working effectively with others, whether humans or other AI agents, to achieve a common goal is increasingly important in complex and dynamic environments. Collaboration requires communication, coordination, and the ability to understand and respect the perspectives of others.
vi. Self-refining: The capacity for self-improvement and adaptation is a hallmark of advanced AI systems. AI agents with self-refining capabilities can learn from experience, adjust their behavior based on feedback, and continuously enhance their performance and capabilities over time. This can involve machine learning techniques, optimization algorithms, or other forms of self-modification.

Agent faculties are the fundamental cognitive and operational capabilities that enable an intelligent agent to interact autonomously with its environment. These faculties are typically organized into five core pillars: Perception, Reasoning, Learning, Decision-making, and Action.
1. Perception (Sensing and Data Ingestion)

Perception is the "input" faculty. It allows an agent to receive raw signals from its environment and transform them into structured information.
i. Multimodal Ingestion: Collecting data from various sources (visual, auditory, textual, or sensor- based).
ii. Symbolic Grounding: Linking raw data to internal categories or concepts, allowing the agent to "understand" what it perceives.
iii. Attention Control: Filtering vast amounts of environmental data to focus only on task-relevant information.
2. Reasoning (Logical Processing and Synthesis)
Reasoning is the "thinking" faculty. It involves processing information to derive new knowledge, make inferences, or create plans.
i. Inference: Using logic or probabilistic models to conclude things that are not directly observed.
ii. Mental Simulation: Predicting the potential outcomes of different scenarios before they occur.
iii. Integration: Coordinating immediate reactive responses with slower, deliberative symbolic logic.
3. Learning (Improving from Experience)
Learning allows an agent to adapt and improve its performance over time without explicit reprograming.
i. Reinforcement: Adjusting behavior based on "reward" or "penalty" signals from the environment.
ii. Episodic Memory: Storing past experiences to recognize and respond better to similar future situations.
iii. Continual Adaptation: The ability to acquire new skills and categories incrementally as the environment changes.
4. Decision-Making (Selecting Actions and Strategies)
Decision-making is the "choice" faculty. It evaluates possible paths and selects the one that best fulfills the agent's goals.
i. Action Selection: Choosing the best behavior based on utility, rules, or priority.
ii. Constraint Satisfaction: Ensuring choices stay within safety, ethical, or resource limits.
iii. Contextual Modulation: Adjusting choices based on internal states (like "goals") or environmental shifts.
5. Action (Execution and Interaction)
Action is the "output" faculty. It is the physical or digital execution of the agent's decisions.
i. Motor/Digital Control: Executing low-level commands (e.g., API calls, robotic movement) to affect the environment.
ii. Closed-Loop Monitoring: Constantly checking the results of an action to see if it was successful or requires correction.
iii. Social Interaction: Coordinating actions with humans or other agents through communication and shared intent.
The Perception-Cognition-Action Loop
In intelligent systems, these faculties do not work in isolation. They form a continuous loop:
i. Perceive the current state.
ii. Reason about the meaning and Learn from the context.
iii. Decide on the optimal response.
iv. Act to change the state, then start again by perceiving the new result.

Agent Rationality
One of the central concepts in Artificial Intelligence is Agent Rationality. AI systems are not judged by how they think, but by how they act. In AI, an agent is considered rational if it does the "right thing."
But what does "right" mean? In AI, right means: Choosing the action that maximizes the expected performance measure, given the information available.
What is a Rational Agent?
A rational agent is an agent that selects actions that maximize its expected success based on:
i. The performance measure
ii. Its percept history
iii. The knowledge it has
iv. The actions available to it
Formal Definition

An agent is rational if for every possible percept sequence, it selects an action that is expected to maximize its performance measure.
Key Elements of Agent Rationality
a. Performance Measure This defines success. Examples:
i. Chess agent ? number of games won
ii. Self-driving car ? safety, speed, fuel efficiency
iii. Spam filter ? classification accuracy
A rational agent always tries to maximize this measure.
b. Percept Sequence
Agents make decisions based on what they have perceived so far. Example:
i. A robot may change direction if it detects an obstacle.
   ii. A medical AI may revise diagnosis after receiving new lab results. Rationality depends on available information, not hidden facts.
c. Prior Knowledge
Some agents are pre-programmed with knowledge about their environment. Example:
i. A GPS system knows road maps.
ii. A chess program knows legal moves.
Better knowledge ? better rational decisions.
d. Available Actions
An agent can only choose among actions it is capable of performing. Example:
i. A robot without arms cannot pick up objects.
ii. A chatbot cannot physically intervene in a situation.

Agent Environment
An agent environment refers to the external setting or world in which an intelligent agent operates. The agent perceives this environment through sensors and acts upon it using actuators. The environment provides the context, states, and constraints that determine the agent's performance and the complexity of its tasks.
Core Properties of Environments
Environments are typically classified according to several key dimensions. Understanding these properties is crucial for selecting the appropriate agent architecture and algorithms.
i. Fully Observable vs. Partially Observable
a. Fully Observable: The agent's sensors can detect the complete state of the environment at each point
in time (e.g., Image of a Chess board).
b. Partially Observable: Parts of the state are missing or noisy, requiring the agent to maintain an internal "belief state" or memory to estimate hidden variables (e.g., A vacuum cleaner with no sensors to detect dirt in other rooms).
ii. Deterministic vs. Stochastic
a. Deterministic: The next state of the environment is completely determined by the current state and the agent's action (e.g., Chess).
b. Stochastic: There is uncertainty; the same action in the same state might lead to different outcomes based on probability (e.g., Driving in traffic).
iii. Episodic vs. Sequential
a. Episodic: The agent's experience is divided into independent "episodes." Actions in one episode do not affect future episodes (e.g., Image classification).
b. Sequential: Current actions have long-term consequences, affecting future states and decisions (e.g., Playing a game of Go).
iv. Static vs. Dynamic
a. Static: The environment does not change while the agent is "thinking" or deliberating (e.g., Crossword puzzles).
b. Dynamic: The environment changes over time or while the agent is deciding on an action (e.g., A taxi driving).

v. Discrete vs. Continuous
a. Discrete: The environment has a finite or countable number of distinct states and actions (e.g., Chess).
b. Continuous: States and actions are defined by real-valued variables (e.g., Steering a car or controlling a robot arm).
vi. Single-Agent vs. Multi-Agent
a. Single-Agent: Only one agent is making decisions in the environment (e.g., A solitary robot in a warehouse).
b. Multi-Agent: Multiple agents interact, which may be cooperative (working together) or competitive (playing against each other).

Agent Architectures
Agent Architecture refers to the underlying framework or structure that defines how an agent is built and how it processes information to produce actions.
In simple terms: Agent = Architecture + Agent Program
a. Architecture ? The hardware or platform
b. Agent Program ? The software running on it

Components of Agent Architecture
An agent architecture typically includes:
i. Sensors
ii. Internal Processing Unit
iii. Knowledge Base
iv. Decision-Making Mechanism
v. Actuators
Basic Agent Structure: Environment ? Sensors ? Processing ? Actuators ? Environment
Types of Agent Architectures
AI systems are classified into different architectures depending on how they process information.
i. Simple Reflex Architecture Description:
a. Acts only on the current percept.
b. Uses condition-action rules (IF-THEN rules).
c. Does not store past information.
Structure: Perception ? Rule Matching ? Action
Example:
a. Thermostat
b. Basic vacuum cleaner agent
Advantages:
a. Simple
b. Fast response
Disadvantages:
a. Fails in partially observable environments
b. No memory
ii. Model-Based Architecture Description:

a. Maintains an internal state (model of the world).
b. Keeps track of past percepts.
Structure: Perception ? Update Internal State ? Decision ? Action
Example:	Robot navigating a room using map memory.
Advantage:	Works in partially observable environments.
iii. Goal-Based Architecture Description:
a. Has explicit goals.
b. Chooses actions that lead to goal achievement.
Structure:	Perception ? State Update ? Goal Evaluation ? Action Selection
Example:	GPS navigation system finding shortest route.
Advantage:	Flexible decision-making.
iv. Utility-Based Architecture Description:
a. Uses a utility function to measure desirability.
b. Selects action that maximizes expected utility.
Structure:	Perception ? Utility Evaluation ? Best Action ? Execution
Example:
a. Stock trading AI maximizing profit.
b. Autonomous car balancing safety and speed.
Advantage:	Handles trade-offs between conflicting goals.
v. Learning Agent Architecture Description:
a. Improves performance through experience.
b. Adapts over time.
Components:
a. Performance Element (selects actions)
b. Learning Element (improves system)
c. Critic (evaluates performance)

SEARCH
Artificial Intelligence is the study of building agents that act rationally. Most of the time, these agents perform some kind of search algorithm in the background in order to achieve their tasks.
In Artificial Intelligence (AI), a search strategy is the method or procedure used to decide which paths or states to explore in order to reach a solution to a problem.
Since many AI problems (like puzzle solving, pathfinding, game playing, or planning) can be represented as a search through a problem space, a strategy helps guide the search efficiently.
Key points:
i. A search strategy defines the order in which nodes (possible states or solutions) are expanded.
ii. It determines which node is chosen first for exploration from the search tree or graph.
 iii. The goal is usually to find a solution that is correct, efficient, and optimal. A search problem consists of:
i. A State Space. Set of all possible states where you can be.
ii. A Start State. The state from where the search begins.
iii. A Goal State. is the desired final condition or outcome that the search algorithm is
trying to reach. It's the state where the problem is considered solved.
The Solution to a search problem is a sequence of actions, called the plan that transforms the start state to the goal state. This plan is achieved through search algorithms.

Problem Solving by Search Components of a search problem A problem in AI is defined by:
a. Initial State - Where the agent starts

b. Actions (Operators) - Possible moves
c. Transition Model - Result of applying an action
d. Goal Test - Determines if goal is achieved
e. Path Cost - Cost of reaching the goal
State Space
The state space is the set of all possible states reachable from the initial state. Search algorithms explore this state space using:
a. Trees
b. Graphs
Example: 8-Puzzle Problem
a. Initial state: Scrambled tiles
b. Goal state: Ordered tiles
c. Actions: Move blank tile up/down/left/right
d. Cost: Number of moves

Types of AI Search Techniques and Strategies
AI search techniques are classified into two main categories:
a. Uninformed (Blind) Search
b. Informed (Heuristic) Search


Uninformed Search Algorithms:
The search algorithms in this section have no additional information on the goal node other than the one provided in the problem definition. The plans to reach the goal state from the start state differ only by the order and/or length of actions. Uninformed search is also called Blind search. These algorithms can only generate the successors and differentiate between the goal state and non-goal state.
  i. Depth First Search	ii. Breadth First Search	iii. Uniform Cost Search Each of these algorithms will have:
i. A problem graph, containing the start node S and the goal node G.
ii. A strategy, describing the manner in which the graph will be traversed to get to G.
iii. A fringe, which is a data structure used to store all the possible states (nodes) that you can go from the current states.
iv. A tree, that results while traversing to the goal node.
v. A solution plan, the sequence of nodes from S to G.
Depth First Search:
Depth-first search (DFS) is an algorithm for traversing or searching tree or graph data structures. The algorithm starts at the root node (selecting some arbitrary node as the root node in the case of a graph) and explores as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. It uses last in- first-

out strategy and hence it is implemented using a stack.

Strategy: Expand the node closest to the goal state, i.e. expand the node with a lower h value.

A* Tree Search
A* Tree Search, or simply known as A* Search, combines the strengths of uniform-cost search and greedy search. In this search, the heuristic is the summation of the cost in UCS, denoted by g(x), and the cost in the greedy search, denoted by h(x). The summed cost is denoted by f(x). Heuristic: The following points should be noted with heuristics in A* search. f(x) = g(x) + h(x)
i. Here, h(x) is called the forward cost and is an estimate of the distance of the current node from the goal node.
ii. And, g(x) is called the backward cost and is the cumulative cost of a node from the root node.
iii. A* search is optimal only when for all nodes, the forward cost for a node h(x) underestimates the actual cost h*(x) to reach the goal. This property of A* heuristic is called admissibility.
Admissibility:	0 = h(x) = h*(x) where:
i. h(x) = heuristic estimate
ii. h*(x) = true cost from node x to the goal
This ensures that A* doesn't "mislead" the search by thinking a path is cheaper than it really is.
Strategy: Choose the node with the lowest f(x) value.

INTRODUCTION TO THE TYPES OF PROBLEMS AND TECHNIQUES IN AI
Fundamental Problem Categories in AI
AI problems can be organized into several recurring categories, each characterized by distinct computational challenges and solution strategies. This taxonomy provides a conceptual framework for understanding the field and guides the selection of appropriate techniques.
i. Search Problems
Search problems involve finding a solution often a sequence of actions or a configuration within a large, discrete space of possibilities. The challenge lies in efficiently exploring this space without exhaustively examining every option.
Characteristics:
a. State space representation: Problems are modeled as states (configurations) and operators (actions that transform one state into another)
b. Goal specification: A clear criterion defines when a solution is found
c. Combinatorial explosion: The number of possible states grows exponentially with problem size, making naive exhaustive search infeasible
Canonical examples:
a. Pathfinding: Finding the shortest route between two locations in a map or graph
b. Puzzle solving: Classic problems like the 8-puzzle, Rubik's cube, or Sudoku
c. Game playing: Searching through possible move sequences in games like chess or Go
d. Constraint satisfaction: Assigning values to variables subject to constraints (e.g., scheduling, resource allocation)
ii. Optimization Problems
Optimization problems seek to find the best solution according to some objective function, often in continuous or very large discrete spaces. Unlike pure search problems, optimization explicitly quantifies solution quality and aims to maximize or minimize this measure.
Characteristics:
a. Objective function: A mathematical function that assigns a numerical score to each candidate solution

b. Constraints: Feasible solutions must satisfy certain conditions
c. Local vs. global optima: Many AI optimization problems involve non-convex landscapes with multiple local optima, making it difficult to find the global optimum
Canonical examples:
a. Parameter tuning: Finding optimal weights in a neural network or coefficients in a regression model
b. Resource allocation: Distributing limited resources to maximize utility or minimize cost
c. Scheduling: Arranging tasks or events to optimize time, cost, or other criteria
d. Design optimization: Configuring systems (e.g., engineering designs, network topologies) to achieve desired performance
Optimization is pervasive in AI, appearing in learning algorithms (minimizing prediction error), planning (maximizing expected reward), and many application domains. Both classical numerical methods and heuristic metaheuristics play important roles
iii. Reasoning and Knowledge Representation
Reasoning problems involve drawing inferences from known facts and rules. This category emphasizes the representation of knowledge in structured, often symbolic, forms and the application of logical or probabilistic inference to derive new conclusions.
Characteristics:
a. Knowledge bases: Collections of facts, rules, and relationships encoded in formal languages
b. Inference mechanisms: Procedures for deriving new knowledge from existing knowledge (e.g., deduction, abduction, induction)
c. Expressiveness vs. tractability trade-off: More expressive representations (e.g., first-order logic) enable richer reasoning but may be computationally expensive
Canonical examples:
a. Theorem proving: Automatically verifying mathematical proofs or logical arguments
b. Expert systems: Encoding domain expertise as rules and using inference engines to provide advice or diagnoses
c. Ontology reasoning: Inferring relationships and classifications in structured knowledge graphs
d. Commonsense reasoning: Drawing everyday inferences that humans perform effortlessly but are challenging for machines
Knowledge representation and reasoning were central to early AI and remain important in domains requiring explainability, formal verification, or integration of symbolic domain knowledge with data- driven methods.

iv. Planning and Decision-Making
Planning problems involve generating sequences of actions to achieve specified goals, often under uncertainty and resource constraints. Decision-making extends planning to scenarios where the agent must choose actions based on incomplete information or probabilistic outcomes.
Characteristics:
a. Action models: Formal descriptions of what actions are available and their effects on the world state
b. Goal-directed behavior: Plans are constructed to transition from an initial state to a goal state
c. Temporal reasoning: Actions unfold over time, and their effects may depend on timing and ordering
d. Uncertainty handling: Real-world planning often requires reasoning about uncertain outcomes and partial observability
Canonical examples:
a. Robot motion planning: Determining collision-free paths and action sequences for robots
b. Automated scheduling: Creating timetables for manufacturing, logistics, or project management

c. Strategic game playing: Planning multi-step strategies in complex games
d. Autonomous systems: Enabling self-driving cars, drones, or spacecraft to plan and execute missions
Planning bridges search and reasoning, often employing heuristic search over action spaces while incorporating domain knowledge and constraints. Modern planning also integrates learning to improve plan quality over time.
v. Learning and Statistical Inference
Learning problems involve acquiring knowledge or improving performance through experience. Rather than being explicitly programmed, learning systems extract patterns, regularities, and predictive models from data.
Characteristics:
a. Data-driven: Learning algorithms require training data	(examples,	observations, or interactions)
b. Generalization: The goal is to perform well on new, unseen data, not just memorize training examples
c. Inductive bias: Learning algorithms embody assumptions about the structure of the problem that guide generalization
Three main paradigms:
Supervised learning: Learning a mapping from inputs to outputs based on labeled training examples. The system is provided with correct input-output pairs and learns to predict outputs for new inputs.
a. Examples: Classification (spam detection, image recognition), regression (price prediction, demand forecasting)
b. Techniques: Decision trees, support vector machines, neural networks
Unsupervised learning: Discovering structure or patterns in data without explicit labels. The system identifies regularities, groupings, or representations in unlabeled data.
a. Examples: Clustering (customer segmentation, document organization), dimensionality reduction, anomaly detection
   b. Techniques: K-means clustering, principal component analysis, autoencoders Reinforcement learning: Learning to make sequential decisions by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or penalties. The agent learns policies that maximize cumulative reward over time.
a. Examples: Game playing (Go, chess, video games), robotics control, resource management
b. Techniques: Q-learning, policy gradient methods, deep reinforcement learning
Learning has become the dominant paradigm in modern AI, driven by the availability of large datasets, powerful computational resources, and algorithmic advances in deep learning.

Core AI Techniques and Methods
Having surveyed the main problem categories, we now examine the principal techniques and methods that AI researchers and practitioners employ to solve these problems. Each technique has characteristic strengths and is particularly well-suited to certain problem types.
i. Heuristic Search Methods
Heuristic search methods explore state spaces intelligently by using domain-specific knowledge (heuristics) to guide the search toward promising regions. These methods are fundamental to solving search and planning problems efficiently.
Key concepts:
a. Heuristic function: An estimate of the cost or distance from a given state to the goal. Good heuristics dramatically reduce search effort
b. Informed vs. uninformed search: Uninformed methods (breadth-first, depth-first) explore systematically without domain knowledge; informed methods (A*, greedy best-first) use heuristics to prioritize exploration

c. Admissibility and consistency: Properties of heuristics that guarantee optimal solutions (for algorithms like A*)
Representative algorithms:
a. A search:* Combines actual cost from the start with heuristic estimated cost to the goal, guaranteeing optimal solutions when the heuristic is admissible
b. Hill climbing and local search: Iteratively move to neighboring states that improve the objective, useful for optimization problems
c. Simulated annealing and genetic algorithms: Metaheuristics that escape local optima through randomization or population-based search
Applications: Heuristic search is widely used in pathfinding (GPS navigation, game AI), puzzle solving, scheduling, and planning. The satisfiability (SAT) problem-determining whether a logical formula can be satisfied-has seen dramatic algorithmic improvements through clever search heuristics, enabling industrial-scale applications.
ii. Logic-Based Systems and Symbolic Reasoning
Logic-based techniques represent knowledge using formal symbolic languages (propositional logic, first-order logic, production rules) and perform reasoning through logical inference. These methods are central to knowledge representation, expert systems, and formal verification.
Key concepts:
a. Propositional and first-order logic: Formal languages for expressing facts and rules with well- defined semantics and inference procedures
b. Production rules: "If-then" rules that encode domain expertise and drive inference engines in expert systems
c. Semantic networks and frames: Structured representations that organize knowledge hierarchically and support inheritance and classification
Representative techniques:
a. Resolution and theorem proving: Automated methods for proving logical consequences
b. Forward and backward chaining: Inference strategies used in rule-based expert systems
   c. Description logics: Formal languages for ontology reasoning and knowledge graphs Applications: Logic-based systems excel in domains requiring explainability, formal correctness, or integration of explicit domain knowledge. Examples include medical diagnosis systems, legal reasoning, configuration systems, and semantic web technologies.
Limitations: Symbolic reasoning can struggle with uncertainty, noisy data, and the brittleness of hand-crafted knowledge bases. The "knowledge acquisition bottleneck"-the difficulty of encoding expert knowledge-has limited the scalability of purely symbolic approaches.
iii. Probabilistic Models and Bayesian Methods
Probabilistic models represent uncertainty explicitly using probability distributions and perform inference using the rules of probability theory. These methods are essential for reasoning under uncertainty and integrating noisy or incomplete information.
Key concepts:
a. Bayesian inference: Updating beliefs about hypotheses in light of observed evidence using Bayes' theorem
b. Graphical	models: Bayesian	networks	and	Markov	networks	represent	conditional independence structure, enabling efficient inference in complex domains
c. Hidden variables: Many probabilistic models include unobserved variables that must be inferred from data
Representative techniques:
a. Bayesian networks: Directed graphical models that encode probabilistic dependencies among variables
b. Hidden Markov Models (HMMs): Models for sequential data with hidden states, widely used in speech recognition and bioinformatics

c. Markov Decision Processes (MDPs): Frameworks for sequential decision-making under uncertainty, foundational to reinforcement learning
d. Probabilistic graphical models: General frameworks encompassing Bayesian networks, Markov random fields, and factor graphs
Applications: Probabilistic methods are used in medical diagnosis (reasoning about diseases given symptoms), robotics (sensor fusion and localization), natural language processing (language models, machine translation), and decision support systems.

3.4 Neural Networks and Deep Learning
Neural networks are computational models inspired by biological neural systems, consisting of interconnected units (neurons) that process information through weighted connections. Deep learning refers to neural networks with many layers, which have achieved remarkable success in perception, language, and game playing.
Key concepts:
a. Artificial neurons: Units that compute weighted sums of inputs and apply nonlinear activation functions
b. Layers and architectures: Networks are organized into layers (input, hidden, output); architecture design is crucial for performance
c. Backpropagation: The algorithm for training neural networks by computing gradients of the loss function with respect to weights
d. Representation	learning: Deep	networks	automatically	learn	hierarchical	feature representations from raw data
Representative architectures:
a. Feedforward networks (MLPs): Basic architecture for classification and regression tasks
b. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): Specialized for image and spatial data, using convolutional layers to capture local patterns
c. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs: Designed for sequential data, maintaining hidden state across time steps
d. Transformers: Attention-based architectures that have revolutionized natural language processing and are increasingly used in other domains
Training paradigms:
a. Supervised learning: Training on labeled data to minimize prediction error
b. Unsupervised learning: Learning representations or generative models from unlabeled data (autoencoders, GANs)
c. Deep reinforcement learning: Combining deep neural networks with reinforcement learning for complex decision-making
Applications: Deep learning has achieved state-of-the-art results in computer vision (image classification, object detection), natural language processing (machine translation, question answering, large language models like ChatGPT), speech recognition, game playing (Go, chess, video games), and many other domains.
Challenges: Despite their success, neural networks face challenges including the need for large labeled datasets, high computational costs, lack of interpretability, vulnerability to adversarial examples, and difficulty incorporating symbolic knowledge or causal reasoning.

Knowledge representation techniques
Knowledge representation (KR) is a fundamental area of artificial intelligence concerned with how to formally encode information about the world in a form that computer systems can utilize to solve complex tasks. The choice of representation formalism profoundly affects what can be expressed, how efficiently reasoning can be performed, and how well the system can handle real-world phenomena such as incompleteness, exceptions, and uncertainty.
Three major paradigms in knowledge representation:
i. Predicate Logic (First-Order Logic): provides a formal, structured language for representing objects, relations, and general rules with precise semantics and sound inference mechanisms.

ii. Non-monotonic Logic: extends classical logic to model defeasible reasoning where conclusions can be withdrawn when new information contradicts earlier assumptions, capturing commonsense reasoning patterns.
iii. Probabilistic Reasoning: represents and reasons about uncertainty by assigning numerical probabilities to propositions, enabling principled belief updating through Bayesian methods.
Each technique addresses different challenges in knowledge representation and offers distinct advantages and trade-offs. Understanding these approaches and their interrelationships is essential for designing intelligent systems that can reason effectively in diverse domains.

Semantic Network
Semantic network is a form of knowledge structuring according to a network of concepts and the relationships among them. Think of it as a map, where one idea is connected to other ideas to signify how they're related, for example, "dog" and "animal."
Semantic networks in AI enable programs to understand and draw conclusions by examining these connections. For example, if a program understands that "dogs are animals" and "animals need food," it can deduce that "dogs need food" as well.
For example; let us consider a university system in which there are various entities connected to each other. Let us represent these relations through nodes and arcs.
i. John is a student.
ii. John pursues computer science.
iii. Computer science is a department.
iv. All departments belong to the university.
v. John owns a laptop.
vi. John owns a Dell laptop.
This above network allows AI systems to draw new conclusions. For example, Since John is studying Computer Science, we can deduce that he is connected to the institution via his department. Since, John has a Dell laptop, its fair to assume he uses it for his academic work.
Advantages of Semantic Network Representation
The key advantages of semantic network representation are as follows -
i. Using natural representations of real-world associations makes it easier to store data and retrieve information.
ii. It allows for inheritance, meaning AI can deduce characteristics (for instance, if all birds can fly, then an eagle is capable of flying).
iii. It's easy to visualize and can be expanded by introducing new concepts and connections.
iv. Supports reasoning by following connections between related concepts.
Disadvantages of Semantic Network Representation
The following are the major disadvantages associated with semantic network representation -
i. As more concepts and relationships are added, the network grows and becomes increasingly difficult to manage.
ii. There is no uniform method for representing relationships, leading to inconsistencies in how knowledge is illustrated.
iii. Finding relationships within a large network can require significant time and computational resources.
iv. Semantic networks have difficulty representing probabilistic or uncertain knowledge when compared to probabilistic models.
Frame Representation
Frame representation offers a method to organize details about objects, events, or concepts. This idea proposes that human memory uses "frames" or "templates" to represent general situations, objects, or events. Each frame has slots (features) and fillers (instances) that describe the traits of what it represents.

For example; let us consider the definition of "car." The frame for a car might have fields for color, model, make, and year. These fields could be filled with instances such as red, Model X, Tesla, and 2023.
Key Components of Frame Representation
The following are the main components of frame representation that help in the successful structuring and organization of knowledge.
i. Frames are structures that hold data representing a specific entity, concept, or situation. They serve as templates for organizing relevant information.
ii. Slots are the properties and attributes of a frame. They describe the characteristics of the entity being represented. For example, in a "person" frame, the slots might include name, age, gender, and occupation.
iii. Fillers are the actual values assigned to these slots. In the case of the "person" frame, John Doe would fill the name slot.
iv. Frames can be merged to illustrate relationships between entities. For instance, a "car" frame might be linked with an "owner" frame to indicate ownership.
v. Some slots may have default values that are assumed unless explicitly stated differently. For example, the default value for the color slot in a "car" frame could be black.
Advantages of Frame representation
Frame representation offers several benefits, which are listed below -
i. It facilitates programming by organizing relevant information in a comprehensible and organized form.
ii. It is flexible and simple to extend, so new features and associations can be introduced with minimal effort.
iii. The visualization is straightforward, and people can easily comprehend how the information is stored and retrievable.
iv. This technique is suitable for many AI applications, including natural language processing and machine vision.
Disadvantages of Frame representation
Despite its usefulness, frame representation has some drawbacks, as mentioned below -
i. Finding the relevant information over many frames can be challenging and time-consuming.
ii. Developing rules to accurately infer new knowledge from frames is difficult, especially for big systems.
iii. Representing highly complex or dynamic knowledge with frames can be difficult because it may necessitate multiple slots and connections.
iv. Frame-based systems are effective for structured knowledge, but they may not handle ambiguous or abstract concepts adequately.

NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a fascinating and vital field within Artificial Intelligence that focuses on the interaction between computers and humans in natural language. It enables machines to understand, interpret, and generate human languages, which is crucial for applications like chatbots, virtual assistants, and automated translation services.
To understand NLP, let's start with the basics. NLP involves several key processes:
1. Tokenization: This is the process of breaking down text into smaller units called tokens, which can be words, phrases, or even characters. Tokenization is the first step in preparing text data for further processing.
2. Stemming and Lemmatization: These techniques reduce words to their root or base form. For example, "running" and "runner" can be reduced to "run". This helps in normalizing text data.
3. Part-of-Speech Tagging: This involves identifying the part of speech of each word in a sentence, such as noun, verb, adjective, etc. This is crucial for understanding the grammatical structure of sentences.

4. Named Entity Recognition (NER): NER identifies and classifies named entities in text into predefined categories such as person names, organizations, locations, medical codes, time expressions, quantities, monetary values, percentages, etc.
5. Sentiment Analysis: This process determines the emotional tone behind a series of words. It is used to gauge the sentiment of a piece of text, whether it's positive, negative, or neutral.
6. Machine Translation: This involves translating text from one language to another using computational models.
In the context of our previous discussions on neural networks, it's important to note that many NLP tasks leverage neural network architectures, especially Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and more recently, Transformer models. These models are designed to handle sequential data, which is a natural fit for text data.
Let's delve into an example to see how these concepts work in practice. Consider the sentence: "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog."
* Tokenization: [The, quick, brown, fox, jumps, over, the, lazy, dog]
* Part-of-Speech Tagging: [DT, JJ, JJ, NN, VBZ, IN, DT, JJ, NN]
* Named Entity Recognition: No named entities in this sentence
* Sentiment Analysis: Neutral sentiment
NLP is not just about processing text; it's about understanding the context and meaning behind the words. This is where the real challenge lies, as language is inherently complex and ambiguous.
As we progress in this course, we'll explore more advanced NLP techniques and their applications. We'll also see how NLP integrates with other AI technologies to create more intelligent and interactive systems.
To get a hands-on understanding, try experimenting with simple NLP tasks using Python libraries like NLTK or spaCy. These libraries provide easy-to-use interfaces for performing various NLP tasks. Remember, the goal of NLP is to make machines understand human language as closely as possible, enabling more natural and effective human-computer interactions.

Natural language understanding (NLU)
NLU enables computers to comprehend human language by breaking it down into hierarchical layers lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic analysis. It uses machine learning to transform unstructured text into structured data, focusing on grammatical structure (syntax) and meaning (semantics). Key techniques include parsing for structure and semantic modeling for interpretation.

Syntactic Structures (Syntax)
Syntax focuses on the grammatical rules, order, and structure of sentences.
i. Parsing: Breaking sentences into constituents (phrases, clauses) to determine relationships,  often using syntax trees to visualize grammatical structure.
ii. Parts-of-Speech (POS) Tagging: Identifying words as nouns, verbs, adjectives, etc..
iii. Subject-Verb-Object (SVO): The basic structural arrangement in many languages.
Semantic Structures (Semantics)
Semantics focuses on the meaning of words, phrases, and sentences in context.
InsightSofa
i. Lexical Semantics: Analyzing the meaning of individual words and their relationships (e.g., synonymy, polysemy).
ii. Compositional Semantics: How smaller units combine to form the meaning of larger expressions.
iii. Semantic Networks: Graph-based structures where nodes represent concepts and edges represent relationships.
iv. Frames: Hierarchical knowledge representations used for modeling complex scenarios or context.
v. Ontologies: Formal definitions of classes and relationships within a specific domain.
NLU Process Layers
i. Lexical Analysis: Breaking down text into tokens (words).
ii. Syntactic Analysis: Checking adherence to grammar.
iii. Semantic Analysis: Ensuring the sentence conveys a logical meaning.
iv. Pragmatic Analysis: Interpreting real-world context, intent, and non-literal components.
Modern Approaches
i. Deep Learning: Using models like RNNs, LSTMs, and CNNs to learn complex language patterns.
ii. Transformers: Models like BERT and GPT utilize attention mechanisms for state-of-the-art understanding of both syntax and semantics.

iii. Hybrid Learning: Combining supervised (labeled data) and unsupervised (unlabeled data) techniques.

INTRODUCTION TO EXPERT SYSTEMS
An expert system is a computer program designed to simulate the decision-making ability of a human  expert. It uses a knowledge base of facts and rules and an inference engine to apply logical rules to  the known facts and deduce new facts. The goal is to provide expert-level solutions, explanations, and recommendations in a specific domain.
Features of Expert Systems
i. High performance in a narrow domain.
ii. Explanation ability (can justify conclusions).
iii. Reasoning under uncertainty (using probabilistic logic or fuzzy logic).
iv. Rule-based or case-based reasoning.
v. User-friendly interfaces.
Basic Components of an Expert System
ComponentDescriptionKnowledge BaseContains domain-specific facts and rules.Inference EngineApplies logical rules to the knowledge base to deduce conclusions.User InterfaceAllows users to interact with the system.
Knowledge Base
It contains domain-specific and high-quality knowledge. Knowledge is required to exhibit intelligence. The success of any ES majorly depends upon the collection of highly accurate and precise knowledge.
Components of Knowledge Base
The knowledge base of an ES is a store of both, factual and heuristic knowledge.
i. Factual Knowledge - It is the information widely accepted by the Knowledge Engineers and scholars in the task domain.
ii. Heuristic Knowledge - It is about practice, accurate judgment, one's ability of evaluation, and
guessing.
Knowledge Acquisition
The success of any expert system majorly depends on the quality, completeness, and accuracy of the information stored in the knowledge base. The knowledge base is formed by readings from various experts, scholars, and the Knowledge Engineers. The knowledge engineer is a person with the

qualities of empathy, quick learning, and case analyzing skills. He acquires information from subject expert by recording, interviewing, and observing him at work, etc. He then categorizes and organizes the information in a meaningful way, in the form of IF-THEN-ELSE rules, to be used by interference machine. The knowledge engineer also monitors the development of the ES.
Inference Engine
Use of efficient procedures and rules by the Inference Engine is essential in deducting a correct, flawless solution. In case of knowledge-based ES, the Inference Engine acquires and manipulates the knowledge from the knowledge base to arrive at a particular solution.
In case of rule-based ES, it:
i. Applies rules repeatedly to the facts, which are obtained from earlier rule application.
ii. Adds new knowledge into the knowledge base if required.
 iii. Resolves rules conflict when multiple rules are applicable to a particular case To recommend a solution, the inference engine uses the following strategies:
i. Forward Chaining
ii. Backward Chaining
Forward Chaining
It is a strategy of an expert system to answer the question, "What can happen next?" Here, the inference engine follows the chain of conditions and derivations and finally deduces the outcome. It considers all the facts and rules, and sorts them before concluding to a solution. This strategy is followed for working on conclusion, result, or effect. For example, prediction of share market status as an effect of changes in interest rates.

Backward Chaining
With this strategy, an expert system finds out the answer to the question, "Why this happened?" On the basis of what has already happened, the inference engine tries to find out which conditions could have happened in the past for this result. This strategy is followed for finding out cause or reason. For example, diagnosis of blood cancer in humans.

User Interface
User interface provides interaction between user of the ES and the ES itself. It is generally Natural

Language Processing so as to be used by the user who is well-versed in the task domain. The user of the ES need not be necessarily an expert in Artificial Intelligence. It explains how the ES has arrived at a particular recommendation. The explanation may be in the following forms:
i. Natural language displayed on screen
ii. Verbal narrations in natural language
iii. Listing of rule numbers displayed on the screen.
The user interface makes it easy to trace the credibility of the deductions.
Requirements of Efficient ES User Interface

ii. It should be designed to work for user's existing or desired work practices.
iii. Its technology should be adaptable to user's requirements; not the other way round.
iv. It should make efficient use of user input.

INTRODUCTION TO COMPUTER IMAGE RECOGNITION.
Image recognition is an application of machine learning (ML) that enables software and devices to identify objects, places, people, writing and actions in digital images or video. Image recognition technology enables computers to identify product defects, helps medical professionals spot anomalies and is integral to the development of autonomous vehicles. Image recognition is a core task of computer vision, the broader field of artificial intelligence (AI) technology that enables software and machines to understand and react to visual data. Engineers use traditional machine learning and deep learning models in image recognition. These approaches are typically separate, and whether they are combined or used independently depends on the specific problem and resource requirements.

Image Recognition is a fundamental task in computer vision which enable machines to identify and categorize objects in images or videos. It involves analyzing pixel data, extracting patterns and classifying visual information using machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) techniques. This capability of Image Recognition is widely used in autonomous vehicles, facial recognition, medical diagnostics, security systems and industrial automation.
Key Components of Image Recognition
i. Image Preprocessing: This is one of the crucial steps in image recognition which enhances image quality and prepares data for feature extraction and classification. It ensures that models receive clean, standardized and optimized images by improving accuracy and efficiency.
ii. Feature Extraction: It is a step in image recognition where meaningful patterns, edges, textures and key points are identified from an image. These extracted features are then used for object detection, classification and pattern recognition. This can be done using traditional computer vision techniques such as edge detection and keypoint descriptors or deep learning- based methods such as Convolutional Neural Networks.
iii. Image classification: This is a fundamental task in computer vision that involves assigning a label or category to an image based on its content. It is widely used in applications such as

face recognition, medical diagnosis, self-driving cars and object detection. There are main approaches to image classification:
a. Traditional Machine Learning-Based Classification using manually extracted features such as SIFT, HOG and ORB.
b. Deep Learning-Based Classification using CNNs, Vision Transformers and pre- trained models
Steps in Image Recognition
To perform Image Recognition we need to follow the steps as mentioned below:
Image Acquisition and Preprocessing
Before analyzing an image, it must be cleaned and prepared. Here are the Preprocessing techniques of Image Recognition:
i. Resizing & Normalization: It is the process of standardizing image size and pixel values.
ii. Data Augmentation: This is a technique used in computer vision to artificially increase the size of a dataset by applying transformations to existing images. The commonly used techniques such as rotation, flipping, cropping and contrast adjustments which helps to improve model robustness.
iii. Noise Reduction: It is the process of removing unwanted distortions i.e., noise from images while preserving important features. Noise can be caused by low-light conditions, sensor imperfections or transmission errors. By using filters like Gaussian blur we can remove image noise.
iv. Color Space Conversion: This is the process of converting RGB images to grayscale when color information is unnecessary.
Feature Extraction
Feature extraction identifies key attributes of an image such as edges, shapes and textures. Following are the methods used to perform Feature Extraction:
i. Edge Detection: This method detects the object boundaries by identifying sudden intensity changes. The commonly used techniques are Canny and Sobel.
ii. Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG): It extracts structural features by computing gradient orientations.
iii. Keypoint Detection: This method detects unique points in an image for object tracking and recognition. The Keypoint Detection can be done by using the techniques such as SIFT, ORB, SURF.
Classification using Machine Learning or Deep Learning
Image classification is the process of assigning a label or category to an image based on its visual features. It is widely used in object detection, medical imaging and facial recognition. Here are the approaches:
i. Machine Learning Approach: This uses handcrafted features such as edges, shapes and textures to classify images.
ii. Deep Learning Approach: This uses neural networks such as CNNs, Transformers to automatically learn features.
Training the Model
Model training is the process of teaching an algorithm to recognize patterns in images by feeding it labeled data. The model learns by adjusting its parameters i.e., weights and biases to minimize classification errors. Here are the training methods -
i. Supervised Learning: In this model the data is trained on labeled datasets i.e., ImageNet, COCO.
ii. Transfer Learning: This model can be trained with the pretrained models and fine-tuning them for specific tasks.
Prediction and Inference
The trained model analyzes new images and predicts object categories based on learned patterns.
